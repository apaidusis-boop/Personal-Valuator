Damodaran valuates high-growth firms with limited profit history by making specific adjustments to the Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) model. According to Damodaran's approach, when dealing with high-growth firms that lack a long-term earnings history, one must consider several factors and make appropriate modifications to ensure accurate valuation [book:chunk 0412].

Firstly, Damodaran suggests using a shorter growth period for the initial phase of high growth. This is because it's unrealistic to assume that a firm can sustain high growth indefinitely. Instead, he recommends estimating how long the firm will be able to maintain these excess returns before competition erodes them [book:chunk 0346].

Secondly, Damodaran advises using a lower growth rate in the stable period than what might initially seem appropriate. The growth rate during this phase should ideally not exceed the nominal growth rate of the economy or even the risk-free rate to reflect realistic expectations about long-term sustainable growth [book:chunk 0374].

Damodaran also emphasizes the importance of considering the firm's size and competitive advantages when estimating its ability to sustain high growth. Smaller firms are more likely to earn excess returns compared to larger ones due to their greater flexibility in market entry and expansion [book:chunk 0346].

Furthermore, Damodaran provides specific tools for valuing high-growth firms, such as the FCFE3st.xls spreadsheet which allows valuation through a temporary period of high growth followed by stable growth. This tool helps in capturing the transition dynamics more accurately than simpler models [book:chunk 0412].

In summary, Damodaran's approach to valuing high-growth firms involves careful consideration of the firm’s size, competitive advantages, and realistic projections for both short-term high growth and long-term stable growth.
