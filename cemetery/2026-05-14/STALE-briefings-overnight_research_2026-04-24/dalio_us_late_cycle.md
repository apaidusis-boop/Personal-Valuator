1. **Interest Rates and Yield Curve**: During an expansionary phase, interest rates are typically low as central banks maintain accommodative monetary policies to support growth. As the economy moves into a late cycle, these rates may start rising due to inflationary pressures or as a preemptive measure by the Federal Reserve to avoid overheating. A flattening yield curve is often seen as an indicator of a potential economic downturn [book:chunk 0291].

2. **Capital Flows**: Strong capital inflows are characteristic of an expansion phase, indicating investor confidence in the economy's growth prospects and stability. Conversely, during a late cycle, there may be signs of weakening capital inflows or even outflows as investors become wary of potential risks [book:chunk 0110].

3. **Inflation**: In an early part of the economic cycle, inflation is usually low and manageable due to strong productivity growth offsetting debt-driven spending. However, in a late cycle, inflationary pressures can build up significantly as demand outstrips supply, leading central banks to tighten monetary policy [book:chunk 012].

4. **Debt Levels**: During an expansion phase, total debt levels relative to GDP tend to rise as businesses and consumers take on more debt to finance growth. In a late cycle, these debt burdens become unsustainable, with debt service payments becoming too large in relation to income, signaling the need for deleveraging [book:chunk 013].

5. **Economic Activity Indicators**: Early signs of economic expansion include robust job creation, manufacturing activity, and consumer spending. As the economy matures into a late cycle, these indicators may start showing signs of fatigue or even decline, despite initial positive data points [book:chunk 0291].
