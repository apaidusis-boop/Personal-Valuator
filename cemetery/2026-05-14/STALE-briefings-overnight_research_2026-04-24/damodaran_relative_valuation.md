### Relative Valuation Methods Preferred by Damodaran: Múltiplos vs DCF

According to Aswath Damodaran in his book "Investment Valuation," the choice between using multiples such as PEG ratios and Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) models depends on the specific characteristics of the firm being analyzed. For high-growth firms, where future cash flows are highly uncertain but expected growth is significant, relative valuation methods like the PEG ratio can be more practical due to their simplicity and ability to capture market sentiment towards growth potential [book:chunk 0531].

However, for mature or stable-growth firms with predictable earnings and cash flows, DCF models provide a more rigorous approach by explicitly discounting future cash flows back to present value using an appropriate cost of capital. This method is particularly useful when the firm's fundamentals are well-understood and can be projected reliably [book:chunk 0532].

### Explanation of PEG Ratio

The Price/Earnings-to-Growth (PEG) ratio is a valuation metric that extends the concept of the PE ratio by incorporating expected growth rates. It is calculated as:

\[ \text{PEG} = \frac{\text{PE Ratio}}{\text{Expected Growth Rate}} \]

Damodaran explains in his book that while the PEG ratio aims to neutralize the effect of growth on valuation, it does not entirely eliminate this impact. Instead, the growth rate becomes more deeply entangled within the multiple [book:chunk 0531]. This means that changes in expected growth rates can significantly affect the PEG ratio, making direct comparisons across firms with different risk profiles and payout ratios challenging.

### Example of Estimating PEG Ratio

Damodaran provides an example to illustrate how to estimate a firm's PEG ratio. For instance, consider a firm with a 25% growth rate in its first five years, followed by an 8% long-term growth rate [book:chunk 0531]. The PEG ratio for this firm based on fundamentals is calculated as 1.15.

### Limitations and Considerations

While the PEG ratio can be a useful tool for comparing firms within similar sectors or industries, it has limitations when applied across different risk levels and growth rates [book:chunk 0532]. Direct comparisons of PEG ratios work only if firms are similar in terms of their growth potential, risk, and payout characteristics. Otherwise, analysts should consider using DCF models for a more precise valuation analysis.

In summary, while the PEG ratio offers a simple way to incorporate expected growth into relative valuation metrics, it is essential to recognize its limitations and use it alongside other methods like DCF when necessary [book:chunk 0532].
