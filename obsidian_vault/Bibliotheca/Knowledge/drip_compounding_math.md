However, generally speaking, the fundamental premises of DRIP compounding include:

1. **Dividend Payments**: Companies pay dividends to shareholders regularly.
2. **Reinvestment Option**: Shareholders have the option to reinvest their dividend payments into purchasing additional shares or fractions thereof.
3. **Growth in Shares Over Time**: As dividends are reinvested, the number of owned shares grows over time, leading to compounded growth.

The sensitivities that could break this thesis include:

1. **Changes in Dividend Yield**: If a company reduces its dividend payments, it can significantly impact the compounding effect.
2. **Market Volatility**: The value of shares can fluctuate due to market conditions, affecting the number of shares acquired through reinvestment.
3. **Company Performance**: Poor financial performance or changes in business strategy may lead to reduced dividends or even their elimination.
