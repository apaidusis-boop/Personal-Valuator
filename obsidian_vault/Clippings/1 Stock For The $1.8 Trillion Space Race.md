The “Space Race” is on.

For years, a range of experts and observers, from astrophysicist Neil deGrasse Tyson to Senator Ted Cruz, have predicted that the first trillionaire will be crowned in space.

Now, with SpaceX’s IPO just weeks away, Barron’s reports that the chances of Elon Musk becoming the world’s first trillionaire are “higher than you think.” Meanwhile, an estimated 16,000 people are expected to ride his coattails to become millionaires.

But it’s not just SpaceX. In fact, our team of analysts here at The Motley Fool are unanimous that one little-known space exploration and rocket company could soar much higher.

Since going public in 2020, it’s already surged by 593%.

But this company, valued at just $38 billion, or around 0.2% the expected valuation for SpaceX, could have much more room to run as it continues to smash records and claim its slice of the $1.8 trillion space commercialization market.

## Record Revenues, Gross Margins, Backlogs, and More

**Rocket Lab Corporation** is a vertically integrated space company headquartered in Long Beach, California. Founded in 2006, the company operates across two main segments: launch services and space systems. It provides end-to-end solutions spanning spacecraft design and manufacturing, optical systems, satellite components, and on-orbit management, in addition to its flagship launch services. Rocket Lab serves commercial, government, and aerospace prime contractor customers across the United States, Japan, Canada, and internationally.

The company is best known for its Electron rocket, a small-lift orbital launch vehicle. But it’s increasingly defined by what comes next: the Neutron, a medium-class reusable rocket intended to compete directly with SpaceX's Falcon 9.

Rocket Lab's financial momentum has been striking.

The company reported record annual revenue for 2025, at $602 million. It also reported a record quarter in Q4 2025, logging $180 million.

At the same time, it achieved record gross margins of 44.3%, signaling that it’s becoming more efficient as its business scales.

The backlog tells an equally compelling story. By the end of 2025, Rocket Lab's backlog had expanded 73% year-over-year to approximately $1.85 billion. Management anticipates around 37% of that figure will convert to revenue within 12 months. The company entered 2026 with roughly $1.1 billion in cash and marketable securities, up from $480 million at the end of 2024—a significant liquidity cushion for a company still in heavy investment mode.

The company's largest single contract to date—an $816 million deal with the Space Development Agency—underscores its credibility as a defense contractor, not merely a commercial launch startup.

### From Launch Provider to Space Prime

The most important element of Rocket Lab's long-term thesis is its evolution from a niche small-satellite launch provider into a diversified space prime. The company is now building full satellites, supplying spacecraft components and subsystems, operating on-orbit management services, and expanding its optical communications capabilities through acquisitions like Mynaric AG. This vertical integration strategy is designed to improve margins, deepen customer relationships, and reduce dependence on any single revenue stream.

### Electron and the Neutron Catalyst

Electron remains the workhorse. Rocket Lab launched 21 times in 2025, including a record seven in Q4. The cadence is accelerating—launches are currently occurring roughly every 11 to 13 days.

But the company's biggest potential catalyst is the Neutron. Designed for medium-class payloads including large constellation deployments and potentially human spaceflight, the Neutron is expected to generate around six times the revenue per launch compared to Electron. Its debut would put Rocket Lab in direct competition with SpaceX's Falcon 9 and open an entirely new market segment for the company.

### Competitive Position

Regulatory barriers to entry in the launch industry are extraordinarily high. The time, cost, and trust required to earn government certification creates a durable moat. Rocket Lab is one of the very few commercial launch providers in the world with a proven orbital track record outside of SpaceX. Investors looking for listed exposure to the private space economy have few alternatives as SpaceX remains private, making RKLB a rare pure-play option.

## Risks

### 1\. Profitability Remains Elusive

Despite strong revenue growth, Rocket Lab posted a net loss of approximately $200 million in 2025. The company is still burning cash at a meaningful rate, primarily due to Neutron development costs. Profitability is not expected in the near term, and sustaining investor confidence through continued losses requires the growth narrative to remain intact.

### 2\. The Neutron Program — Execution Risk

Neutron is both the biggest upside catalyst and the biggest single risk. The program has been delayed repeatedly — from an initial 2024 target to late 2025, and now to mid-2026 at the earliest. A test failure in early 2026 triggered a notable stock selloff. Further delays, engine issues, or integration problems could force another timeline revision and trigger a significant re-rating. In an extreme scenario, a failed Neutron program would threaten the company's long-term trajectory.

### 3\. Stretched Valuation

RKLB trades at an exceptionally high revenue multiple, north of 50x trailing twelve-month sales as of early 2026. The stock trades at approximately 29.5x book value, versus roughly 4x for the broader US aerospace and defense sector.

This kind of premium leaves virtually no margin for error: any missed milestone, earnings shortfall, or macro headwind could produce an outsized move to the downside. The stock has already fallen more than 40% from its January 2026 peak.

### 4\. Competition

SpaceX's dominance in launch is entrenched and expanding. On the horizon, emerging rivals — including United Launch Alliance and international competitors — are investing in similar capabilities. Rocket Lab's Electron faces competition in the small-satellite launch market, while Neutron, once operational, will face Falcon 9 head-on. Technological change in the sector moves rapidly and could render current offerings less competitive.

### 5\. Concentration and Dependency Risks

A meaningful portion of Rocket Lab's revenue is tied to government contracts, particularly with the U.S. Department of Defense and agencies like NASA. Changes in federal budget priorities, contract cancellations, or shifts in defense spending could materially impact revenue. Additionally, the company still depends on a relatively limited number of large customers.

### 6\. High Volatility

RKLB's weekly volatility is higher than approximately 75% of all U.S.-listed stocks. The stock's 52-week range from its recent low of $14.71 to a high of $99.58 illustrates the dramatic swings investors must be prepared to endure.

## Analyst Consensus and Valuation

As of late March 2026, RKLB trades near $61 per share with a market capitalization of approximately $41 billion. The consensus analyst price target stands near $89–90, implying roughly 47% upside from current levels. Even the most pessimistic analyst target of $68 sits above the current price, suggesting the sell-off may have created an entry opportunity — though that view must be weighed heavily against the valuation risks described above.

## Bottom Line

Rocket Lab is a genuinely exciting business at an inflection point. The revenue growth is real, the backlog is substantial, the competitive moat is meaningful, and the market opportunity, spanning small and medium launches, satellite manufacturing, and defense systems, is enormous. For long-term growth investors willing to tolerate high volatility and the possibility of further near-term downside, RKLB represents one of the most compelling ways to gain listed exposure to the private space economy.

That said, this is an early-stage, deeply speculative investment by conventional metrics. The company is not profitable, its most important product is unproven at scale, its valuation demands near-flawless execution, and the share price can move violently on one-day news events. Investors should consider risk management strategies such as dollar-cost-averaging, whereby they establish a position slowly over time, while never risking more than they can comfortably afford to lose.
