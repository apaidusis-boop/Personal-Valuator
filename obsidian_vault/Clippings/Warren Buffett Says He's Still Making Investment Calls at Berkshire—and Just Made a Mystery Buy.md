### Key Takeaways

- Warren Buffett told *CNBC* in a televised interview Tuesday that he still comes into the office daily and makes investment decisions at Berkshire Hathaway, after handing the CEO title to Greg Abel at the start of this year.
- He called the recent market sell-off "nothing to get excited about," pointing out that Berkshire has weathered three drawdowns exceeding 50% during his 60 years at the helm.

So much for the [Oracle of Omaha](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/o/oracleofomaha.asp) 's retirement.

Three months after [handing over his role as CEO](https://www.investopedia.com/the-buffett-era-as-ceo-is-over-here-is-how-much-he-crushed-the-market-berkshire-stock-11878302) of Berkshire Hathaway ([BRK.A](https://www.investopedia.com/markets/quote?tvwidgetsymbol=BRK.A), [BRK.B](https://www.investopedia.com/markets/quote?tvwidgetsymbol=BRK.b)) to Greg Abel, the 95-year-old investor told *CNBC* in a televised interview Tuesday that he still comes into the office every day, decides about major investments, and recently bought something new—though he declined to say what.1

"I can still contribute a tiny bit," Buffett said.

Clearly, stepping back from the top job hasn't meant stepping back from the market. Buffett said he calls Mark Millard, Berkshire's director of financial assets, each morning before the opening bell to talk through what's moving. The trades follow those conversations.

But he's keeping Abel in the loop. "I won't make any \[investments\] that [Greg \[Abel\]](https://www.investopedia.com/financial-edge/1211/introducing-warren-buffetts-successor.aspx) thinks are wrong," Buffett said. "Greg gets the sheet every day," he said, referring to Berkshire's daily record of investment activity.

### Why This Matters To You

Buffett's calm, at a time when many investors have been rattled by the market's recent volatility, speaks to something central to Buffett's [approach to investing](https://www.investopedia.com/articles/01/071801.asp): keeping a focus on the long-term, not short-term fluctuations.

## Still Sitting on a Mountain of Cash

Berkshire's cash, cash equivalents, and short-term investments totaled $373 billion at the end of 2025, with much of it already in.

Berkshire bought about $17 billion in U.S. Treasury bills at this week's auction.2 The move suggests that while many stocks may be [looking cheap](https://www.investopedia.com/bill-ackman-called-u-s-stocks-extremely-cheap-markets-wavered-what-s-next-for-investors-11937381) lately, Buffett isn't rushing to put that money to work.

"No," was his curt answer when asked if he's been finding bargains after the market's recent drop from [January's record highs](https://www.investopedia.com/dow-jones-today-01272026-11893188). The major indexes climbed Tuesday, but were still set to record their [worst quarterly performance](https://www.investopedia.com/the-stock-market-is-having-its-worst-quarter-in-years-and-some-pretty-rough-days-can-it-turn-around-11937450) in nearly four years.

But Buffett said he's not going to sit on the sidelines if better opportunities come up. "If there is a big decline" in the stock market, Buffett said, "we will deploy."

For those looking anxiously at their [401(k)s](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/1/401kplan.asp) lately, Buffett's message was to remain calm.

He waved off the recent market as historically mild. "Three times since I took over, for sure it's gone down more than 50%," he said. "This is nothing to make you get excited."

Buffett took control of Berkshire in 1965. He's watched [markets collapse](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/stock-market-crash.asp) in 1974, 1987, 2000–2002, and 2008–2009. By comparison, this year's " [correction](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/c/correction.asp) " in the Dow still pales.
