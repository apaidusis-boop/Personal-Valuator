When the CEO of OpenAI, a legendary hedge fund billionaire, and the Fed Chair all start hinting at the same thing… we pay attention.

Sam Altman, who helped spark this entire boom, recently said:

*“Are we in a phase where investors as a whole are overexcited about AI? My opinion is yes.”*

Ray Dalio, founder of Bridgewater Associates, sees it too, calling this moment “very similar” to the dotcom bubble of the late 90s and early 2000s.

Even Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell recently acknowledged that:

*“Equity prices are fairly highly valued.”*

For a Fed Chair, that’s as close to “careful now” as it gets.

And behind the scenes? Meta is pausing AI hiring. MIT says 95% of GenAI projects are failing inside companies.

Even Google DeepMind’s co-founder said the explosion of funding into generative AI is starting to look a lot like the crypto boom, complete with inflated expectations.

**We don’t bring all this up to scare you out of AI.**

Far from it.

We believe AI is one of the most pivotal, transformational technologies of our lifetimes.

That’s why we’ve published multiple in-depth reports on AI. Why we’ve recommended companies like Nvidia, Alphabet, AMD, and ASML, years before AI was mainstream.

But even with all that conviction, we also believe in staying grounded, especially when the market starts leaning too far in one direction.

As Motley Fool CEO Tom Gardner recently said in a quarterly call:

*“Even artificial intelligence, as with every other innovation in history, can become overvalued”*

He’s also pointed out that today’s valuations across many U.S. equities are running rich, and that now is a good moment to pause, assess, and in his words, *“take one step closer toward risk management.”*

That doesn’t mean abandoning innovation.

It means **diversifying your holdings and investing across multiple sectors**, so your portfolio is positioned for the future, without being overexposed to a single theme.

That’s what this report is about.

While many of our recommendations are tied to the AI trend, this report is intentionally different.

It’s designed to give investors an opportunity to diversify, to own high-quality businesses that aren’t dependent on AI headlines or sector enthusiasm to thrive.

These are companies with real revenues, durable advantages, and business models that can weather downturns, changing narratives, and shifts in market focus.

In the pages that follow, you’ll find six of them.

And while they may not be grabbing headlines like the latest AI startup... they might just offer something even more valuable right now: stability, durability, and most importantly, diversity.

## 1\. COSTCO (NASDAQ: COST)

Costco is the kind of business that just works.

It delivers low prices, loyal customers, and consistent results. Memberships provide access to deeply discounted groceries, household goods, and everyday essentials. And through good economies and bad, the model holds up.

While AI companies grab headlines with vaporware and burn rates, Costco sells more than 150 million rotisserie chickens a year and generates billions in membership fees alone. That’s the kind of recurring, high-margin revenue most software startups would kill for.

Renewal rates in the U.S. exceed 90 percent. Globally, they’re close. That kind of stickiness is rare in retail, and it reflects the trust Costco has built by passing savings directly to members. The company famously caps most markups at around 14 to 15 percent, far below industry averages.

It’s also quietly become a meaningful e-commerce player, with online sales now contributing a growing share of revenue. At the same time, Costco continues to expand its physical footprint, opening 20 to 30 new warehouses each year across both domestic and international markets.

The typical Costco shopper earns around $125,000 annually. That gives the business a high-quality base of customers who tend to keep spending, even when the economy tightens.

Costco isn’t in the business of chasing hype. It’s in the business of keeping people happy, saving them money, and delivering steady growth over time. That makes it a valuable anchor in any long-term portfolio.

## 2\. TJX COMPANIES (NYSE: TJX)

TJX is one of the best businesses most investors overlook. It owns brands like T.J. Maxx, Marshalls, HomeGoods, and Winners, delivering name-brand clothing, home furnishings, and essentials at prices traditional retailers can’t match.

And while other chains struggle with inventory or discounting, TJX thrives in precisely that environment.

That’s because its entire model is built around opportunistic buying. Over 1,300 professional buyers are constantly on the hunt for excess inventory and markdowns from brands and suppliers, allowing TJX to offer deals year-round without running heavy promotions.

The result is a business that performs well when consumers are spending freely, and arguably even better when they start pulling back. When shoppers look to stretch their dollars, they often turn to off-price retail.

That resilience shows up in the numbers. While many retailers took a hit during recent market pullbacks, TJX stock has proven durable. In fact, when the S&P 500 dropped nearly 19 percent in early 2025, TJX shares declined just 5 percent.

With over 5,000 stores and plans to grow that number significantly, especially through its fast-expanding HomeGoods segment and European operations, TJX still has room to run.

It’s a cash-generating, margin-resilient, recession-tested retailer with decades of performance behind it.

And in a market increasingly dominated by sentiment shifts and sector rotations, TJX offers something refreshingly simple: a well-run business selling things people actually want to buy, at prices they can afford.

## 3\. STARBUCKS (NASDAQ: SBUX)

Starbucks has become part of daily life for millions of people around the world.

With more than 18,000 stores in North America and a growing international footprint, it’s one of the most recognizable and trusted brands in modern retail. But Starbucks’ real strength isn’t just its scale; it’s the loyalty and routine it builds with customers.

For many, a stop at Starbucks isn’t a splurge. It’s a habit. And that habit drives consistent traffic, pricing power, and reliable revenue across economic cycles.

Under CEO Brian Niccol, the company has been refocusing on what made the brand successful in the first place. That’s meant simplifying the menu, doubling down on in-store experience, and restoring Starbucks’ identity as a welcoming, high-touch “third place” between home and work.

Niccol has also shown a willingness to make hard decisions including closing underperforming stores, retraining teams, and streamlining operations. At the same time, Starbucks continues to invest in digital ordering, loyalty programs, and high-growth international markets like China and India.

There are challenges ahead. Rising labor costs, store efficiency, and global volatility will all test the business. But Starbucks has a long history of navigating change, often by getting closer to the customer, not further away.

It’s a brand built on habit, trust, and millions of repeat visits every single day. And we think that foundation gives Starbucks the durability most companies only hope for.

## 4\. CASEY’S GENERAL STORE (NASDAQ: CASY)

If you’ve ever driven through small-town America, you’ve likely stumbled across a Casey’s.

The company operates nearly 3,000 convenience stores, serving up fuel, groceries, and grab-and-go food to communities often overlooked by bigger national chains. And it’s not just selling snacks and gas; Casey’s is the fifth-largest pizza chain in the U.S.

That food business is a quiet profit engine. Prepared food and beverage margins have recently topped 50%, helping to support healthy overall returns even when fuel margins compress.

Casey’s has also grown steadily through smart, well-priced acquisitions. In late 2024, Casey’s purchased nearly 200 CEFCO gas stations across multiple states, continuing its steady expansion without overreaching. The company plans to open at least 80 more stores in fiscal 2026, keeping growth focused and manageable.

Unlike many retailers, Casey’s owns much of its supply chain, including three major distribution centers and a majority of its fuel delivery trucks. That vertical integration gives it better cost control, faster response times, and more flexibility to reinvest in customer experience.

Since 1990, Casey’s stock has outperformed the S&P 500 by a factor of nearly 8 to 1. That kind of compounding comes not from hype, but from a business model that works, and keeps working.

For investors looking to balance out high-growth tech or AI exposure, Casey’s offers something very different: steady demand, loyal customers, and a business that makes money every single day.

## 5\. WASTE MANAGEMENT (NYSE: WM)

In a world chasing breakthroughs and moonshots, Waste Management is built on something far more reliable: essential services with steady, recurring demand.

The company is the largest provider of waste collection, disposal, and recycling services in North America. It serves municipalities, businesses, and households through long-term contracts, many of which include built-in price escalators that adjust with inflation.

That gives Waste Management a powerful advantage: steady, predictable cash flow in just about every economic environment.

It also benefits from a unique form of protection. Waste is a local business, heavily regulated and deeply embedded in local infrastructure. That creates high barriers to entry and gives WM something rare in any industry: territorial dominance.

But this isn’t just a legacy trash hauler. Over the past few years, Waste Management has invested heavily in automation, building high-efficiency recycling facilities that generate stronger margins than their manual counterparts. It’s also expanding into renewable energy by capturing landfill gas and converting it into fuel.

It’s worth noting that it carries debt. And yes, parts of the business, especially commercial and construction-related activity, can be cyclical. But Waste Management’s core services are essential and inelastic. People don’t cancel trash service when the economy slows.

As long as people still have trash to throw away, Waste Management will stay in demand.

## 6\. MERCADOLIBRE (NASDAQ: MELI)

MercadoLibre is what Amazon, PayPal, and Shopify might look like if they were rolled into one, and rebuilt for Latin America.

It started as an online marketplace. But over the years, it’s quietly built an entire ecosystem: e-commerce, logistics, payments, lending, even advertising. That tight integration has helped MercadoLibre defend its position as the region’s most dominant digital platform.

Its fintech arm, Mercado Pago, now processes more money than the marketplace brings in through merchandise. Its shipping and logistics unit, Mercado Envios, can move products more efficiently than most competitors in the region. And with millions of loyal users already on the platform, its advertising business is scaling fast.

The opportunity is massive. In many Latin American countries, credit card usage is still low, digital commerce is underpenetrated, and traditional banking options are expensive or hard to access. That creates a long runway for MercadoLibre to bring more users into its orbit.

However, it’s not without risks. Latin America brings currency volatility, political instability, and inflation pressures. But MercadoLibre has a strong track record of operating across borders and adapting to shifting environments.

This isn’t a household name for most U.S. investors. But it’s a mission-critical platform for millions of users across Latin America.

MercadoLibre stands out on this list, and is a favorite among many of our analysts, because it offers high-growth tech potential without relying on AI hype to drive its stock price. That makes it a smart way to stay in tech without adding more exposure to an already crowded trade.
