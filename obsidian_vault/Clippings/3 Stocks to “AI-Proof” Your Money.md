Here are 3 stocks we believe are uniquely positioned to thrive even as the $15.7 trillion AI Revolution lays waste to other industries.

In February 2026, a thought experiment of what AI could do to software companies, private equity firms, and other sectors caused stocks to reel. Shares of International Business Machines (IBM) fell by 13%, in the stock’s worst performance since the Dot.com collapse in 2000. Financial firms and software-as-a-service (SaaS) stocks plummeted in what has been termed the “Saas-pocalypse.”

Interestingly, this blog post wasn’t even a prediction, just a thought experiment. But Wall Street’s violent shudder just at the thought of this no doubt echoes what many individual investors are feeling.

Right now, no sector seems safe from AI disruption — not even Big Tech, which has seen selloffs in recent months as tech giants like Microsoft (MSFT) and Nvidia (NVDA) fail to live up to sky-high expectations. When Microsoft can report profit growth of 60%, as it did in February 2026, and still crash 10% anyway, what company is safe in the Age of AI?

Our team has spent considerable resources to address this very question, as it might be the most important one to get right for millions of investors as they navigate the disruption that’s still to come.

Here are 3 stocks with unique brands, industry positions, and other advantages that should allow them to not just survive but thrive in the years ahead.

## 1\. Costco: Built to Resist AI Disruption

As artificial intelligence reshapes industries, investors are increasingly sorting companies into two camps: those threatened by AI and those insulated from it. For Costco Wholesale, the evidence points firmly toward the latter.

Costco is a “battleship business” with a track record of surviving tough or uncertain conditions. It has raised its dividend each year since 2004, amounting to an overall increase of 1,200%\* that easily outpaces the 75% inflation seen in that period.

As powerful as this record is, it doesn’t even include management’s tradition of paying out special dividends every few years. In 2015, 2017, 2020, and 2024, the company paid out special dividends that were at least 1,300% greater than the regular quarterly ones.

Since ChatGPT kicked off the Age of AI in November 2022, Costco shares have returned 90%. In its last earnings report, it posted earnings growth of 13.8% while revenues climbed 9.2%.

Why is Costco so resilient at a time when mere rumors of disruption send ripples through Wall Street?

The answer has to do with the warehouse retail giant's business model, which is built around physical scale, member loyalty, and relentless cost discipline. It contains structural defenses against AI disruption that most companies simply can’t replicate.

### The AI Disruption Landscape

The broader market is grappling with genuine anxiety. Mentions of AI disruption on S&P 500 earnings calls nearly doubled in early 2026 compared to the prior quarter, and software stocks have been hit particularly hard on fears that AI will commoditize their products overnight.

The question for investors is: Does Costco belong in that conversation? A close look at what makes Costco tick suggests the answer is no — and for reasons that are structural rather than simply cyclical.

### A Business Model AI Can't Replace

At its core, Costco is not a software company or an information business. It is a physical logistics operation wrapped in a membership model. Its value proposition rests on three pillars: buying goods in extreme bulk, negotiating prices competitors cannot match, and passing those savings to paying members.

None of these pillars are easily disrupted by AI. An AI shopping agent can help a consumer compare prices across websites. But it can’t manufacture the supplier relationships Costco has built over decades, nor replicate its 60%-plus share of the warehouse club market. Costco's massive scale makes competitive disruption difficult — it pulls in triple the annual revenue of its closest rival, Sam's Club, and carries enormous leverage in procurement negotiations that no algorithm can simply conjure.

The membership model adds another layer of insulation. With over 136 million cardholders worldwide and a membership renewal rate above 90%, Costco has one of the most loyal customer bases in retail. That loyalty is not driven by an app or a recommendation engine — it’s driven by the lived experience of walking into a warehouse and finding genuine value. That experience is inherently physical, social, and habitual.

### Recurring Revenue: The Investor's Shield

One of the most compelling aspects of Costco's AI-resistance is financial, not operational. Unlike traditional retailers that live and die by merchandise margins, Costco derives almost all of its profit from membership fees. The retail operation itself runs at near-breakeven by design — the fees are the business. This means even if AI agents were to nibble at some of Costco's product categories or accelerate price comparison by consumers, the revenue stream that actually moves the needle, membership fee income, is barely touched.

This structure makes Costco's earnings both predictable and difficult to disintermediate. Membership revenue renews annually and behaves more like a subscription business than a retail one, providing a financial buffer that most competitors lack.

### Costco Uses AI — On Its Own Terms

What makes Costco's story even more compelling for investors is that it is not passively sitting out the AI revolution. It is actively deploying AI where AI actually helps its model, rather than being disrupted by it.

The approach is deliberately backend-first. Costco's CFO has been explicit about focusing on "foundational improvements" over flashy customer-facing technology. The results are real: an early machine learning model for predicting daily bakery demand across 30 warehouses saved the company nearly $100 million and was subsequently rolled out company-wide. AI-driven route optimization now adjusts delivery logistics in real time, accounting for traffic, weather, and fuel efficiency. Autonomous drones conduct overnight inventory counts with near-perfect accuracy, eliminating both hazardous manual labor and operational disruption to daytime warehouse activity. IoT sensors and RFID tags give managers live dashboards of stock positions across the entire supply chain.

In each case, AI is being used to tighten the operational machine that keeps prices low — which is exactly what the membership model requires. For a business running on 3–4% operating margins, shaving costs through AI in logistics, inventory, and labor scheduling is not a luxury. It is essential to the flywheel.

### The Physical Moat Is Durable

Perhaps the deepest protection Costco has against AI disruption is the one that gets the least attention: the sheer physical reality of its warehouses. Costco operates over 900 locations worldwide, and its model depends on members driving to stores, buying in bulk, and loading up their cars. This is not a bug — it is the feature. The treasure hunt experience, the famous $1.50 hot dog, the free samples, the sense of discovery — these are sensory, physical experiences that no AI-powered e-commerce interface has come close to replicating.

Amazon has tried to crack the warehouse club model for years and has not meaningfully dented Costco's membership base. If the world's most sophisticated e-commerce and AI company cannot disrupt Costco at scale, the disruption risk from AI alone looks modest.

### Risks Worth Acknowledging

No investment thesis is complete without the counterargument. Costco's institutional culture — famously people-centric, promote-from-within — can be slow to adapt to new technology. Competitors like Sam's Club and Amazon are investing aggressively in frictionless, digitally integrated shopping experiences. If AI shopping agents eventually reduce the friction of online bulk purchasing to the point that consumers stop making the trip, Costco's e-commerce capabilities — still well behind Amazon and Walmart — could become a liability.

The tariff environment in early 2026 also presents a near-term headwind, with Costco among the retailers seeking reimbursement for tariff-related cost increases. And while e-commerce now represents a growing share of revenue, the company still lags its largest peers in digital infrastructure.

### The Bottom Line

Costco is not immune to change, but it is exceptionally well-positioned to survive an AI-driven retail transformation without structural damage to its core business. Its membership revenue is recurring and defensible, its supplier leverage is irreplaceable, its physical experience is genuinely difficult to replicate online, and its own AI investments are already paying off in the margins that matter most.

In a market where AI disruption is increasingly being priced into stock valuations across sectors, Costco's resilience looks not just like a safe haven, but a competitive advantage in its own right.

## 2\. Comfort Systems (FIX): The Pick-and-Shovel Play AI Can't Disrupt

When ChatGPT launched in November 2022, most investors looked to chipmakers, cloud platforms, and software companies for the windfall. Few looked at an HVAC contractor headquartered in Houston, Texas.

Comfort Systems USA traded at roughly $115 per share when ChatGPT went live. As of March 2026, it trades above $1,400. This gain of more than 1,100% in roughly 28 months puts most pure-play AI stocks to shame. And it’s delivered by a company that installs heating, cooling, and electrical systems in buildings.

The story of how that happened, and why it is likely to continue, shows why FIX is among the most AI-resilient stocks on the market.

### What Comfort Systems Actually Does

Comfort Systems is a mechanical and electrical contracting company. Its 45-plus subsidiaries design, install, and maintain HVAC systems, plumbing, piping, electrical infrastructure, and building automation systems for commercial, industrial, and institutional clients across the United States. Revenue in 2025 reached $9.1 billion, up nearly 30% from the year prior. Earnings surged 96% in the same period.

The company operates in two segments — Mechanical and Electrical — and has increasingly leaned into modular, off-site construction, with modular revenue now representing roughly 18% of total sales. It serves data centers, semiconductor fabs, healthcare facilities, and manufacturing plants: exactly the industries pouring capital into physical infrastructure.

### The AI Tailwind Nobody Talked About

Here is the central irony that defines FIX's investment thesis: the more powerful AI becomes, the more Comfort Systems benefits. Every large language model, every AI inference cluster, every hyperscale data center requires massive amounts of cooling infrastructure, high-density power systems, and precision climate control. These are not optional upgrades. They are physical prerequisites. Without them, the servers overheat and fail.

AI systems generate extraordinary amounts of heat. The shift toward high-density GPU compute has made thermal management one of the most demanding engineering challenges in modern construction. Comfort Systems is one of a small number of companies with the workforce, technical expertise, and modular fabrication capability to deliver these systems at scale — and its $9.4 billion backlog as of Q3 2025 reflects the depth of that demand. Backlog has grown roughly 34% over two years, with technology and manufacturing clients driving the bulk of new awards.

In its Q4 2025 earnings report, the company posted EPS of $9.37 against analyst expectations of $6.77 — a beat of nearly 40%. The electrical segment, which handles data center power infrastructure, has been particularly explosive, with revenue climbing 71% year over year at peak.

### Why We Think AI Cannot Disrupt This Business

The disruption risk from AI is concentrated in industries where the core product is information, software, or repeatable cognitive tasks. Comfort Systems operates in a fundamentally different category: skilled physical labor, applied in complex, site-specific environments.

Installing a cooling system in a hyperscale data center is not a task that can be automated away. It requires licensed tradespeople, on-site engineering judgment, coordination across dozens of subcontractors, and deep familiarity with local building codes. No large language model can tighten a pipe fitting or commission a chiller. The skilled trades shortage in the United States, if anything, makes Comfort Systems' workforce and operational expertise more valuable over time, not less.

There is also a powerful network effect embedded in the business. Comfort Systems has spent nearly three decades building relationships with general contractors, developers, and institutional clients. Its subsidiaries carry deep local knowledge and reputations that cannot be replicated by a new entrant or disrupted by a software platform. These relationships produce the backlog; the backlog produces the revenue visibility; the revenue visibility is what commands a premium valuation.

### A Stock Born for This Moment

What makes FIX unusual in the current environment is that it sits at the intersection of two of the most powerful investment themes of the decade — AI infrastructure buildout and domestic manufacturing reshoring — without carrying the valuation risk of a pure-play technology company.

Its scale means it now commands contracts that smaller competitors cannot bid on. It joined the S&P 500 in late 2024, bringing institutional buying flows that further reinforced the stock's trajectory. Analyst price targets have been revised sharply upward, with targets ranging from $1,400 to $1,800 as of early 2026.

### A Note of Caution

No report would be complete without acknowledging the risks. FIX is not a cheap stock. It trades at a significant premium to its historical price-to-earnings averages, and some analysts have flagged that the current valuation assumes years of continued double-digit earnings growth. A slowdown in data center construction spending could weigh on the stock meaningfully. Labor shortages remain a structural constraint on how quickly the company can scale.

### The Bottom Line

Comfort Systems USA has delivered one of the most remarkable performances of the AI era without writing a single line of code. It has done so by being indispensable to the physical world that AI requires to function. While the market has spent three years debating which software companies AI will make obsolete, FIX has quietly become the company that makes AI possible… and investors who recognized that early have been richly rewarded.

For the AI era to continue, data centers must be built, cooled, and powered. That makes Comfort Systems not a casualty of disruption, but one of its greatest beneficiaries.

## 3\. Vertex Pharmaceuticals (VRTX): How AI Is Powering Its Next Chapter

There is a particular kind of company that thrives not just because it adopts new technology, but because the technology solves its most expensive problems. For Vertex Pharmaceuticals, artificial intelligence is doing exactly that. It’s compressing the timelines, cost, and uncertainty that define drug development, and in doing so, expanding the boundaries of what this Boston-based biotech can realistically achieve.

### From Cystic Fibrosis Champion to Multi-Disease Powerhouse

Vertex built one of the most enviable franchises in all of biotech by cracking the code on cystic fibrosis. Its TRIKAFTA therapy became one of the most transformative medicines in recent memory, and its CF portfolio now generates revenues that few specialty biotechs can rival. For 2025, Vertex logged revenue of $12 billion in 2025, and for 2026, it is projecting $12.95 billion to $13.1 billion, with more than $500 million expected from non-CF products, a revenue source that barely existed two years ago.

That diversification is no accident. It’s the result of a company using its CF cash engine to systematically fund AI-assisted drug discovery across entirely new disease territories — pain management, kidney disease, Type 1 diabetes, gene editing, and beyond. AI has not just improved Vertex's efficiency; it has expanded its ambition.

### The AI Toolkit: Where It's Actually Being Used

Vertex's application of AI runs across the full drug development lifecycle, not just in headline-grabbing data science projects.

In target identification, machine learning models analyze vast genomic and proteomic datasets to pinpoint which proteins and genes are most likely involved in disease pathways. This is work that previously required years of manual lab experimentation. AI condenses it to weeks.

In lead optimization — the process of selecting which compounds to advance — Vertex has built a proprietary MLOps platform using AWS-native infrastructure, including Amazon SageMaker, to generate real-time predictive models of molecular activity. Rather than physically testing thousands of compounds for biological activity and toxicity, the system generates quantitative predictions in a fraction of the time and cost. This approach has been central to accelerating the company's pipeline expansion.

In clinical trials, AI improves both the design and analysis of studies. Biomarker identification powered by machine learning helps Vertex predict which patient populations will respond to a given therapy, enabling more targeted trial design and reducing the risk of expensive late-stage failures.

Then there is Vertex's long-running collaboration with Genomics plc, a partnership established in 2018 and subsequently extended, which uses large-scale human genetic data and machine learning to improve the identification of precision medicine targets. This is a longer-horizon bet using population genetics as a training set to identify disease drivers that conventional research might never surface.

### CASGEVY: A Real-World AI Data Engine

Perhaps the most strategically underappreciated aspect of Vertex's AI story is CASGEVY, the CRISPR gene-editing therapy it co-developed with CRISPR Therapeutics for sickle cell disease and beta thalassemia. CASGEVY generated over $100 million in revenue in 2025 and is in active global rollout, with more than 65 authorized treatment centers operating worldwide.

Beyond the revenue, however, CASGEVY functions as a living data-generation machine. Each patient treated produces extraordinarily rich, high-fidelity real-world datasets — the kind of proprietary biological information that is both rare and enormously valuable for training AI drug discovery models. As AI's effectiveness scales with data quality, Vertex is quietly accumulating one of the most valuable datasets in precision medicine. Competitors cannot simply buy or replicate it.

### The CEO Comes Around

In November 2025, Vertex CEO Reshma Kewalramani made a candid admission that resonated across the biotech sector. She told attendees at a Boston College Chief Executives Club event that she used to roll her eyes at questions about AI in drug development "maybe even up to a year ago." The conversion of a prominent biotech CEO from skeptic to believer, voiced publicly, shows how quickly the technology's utility has become undeniable at the lab bench.

Kewalramani noted that AI now has the potential to compress what were months-long drug approval documentation processes down to minutes. That efficiency gain, applied across a pipeline with multiple late-stage programs simultaneously, has material financial consequences.

### Stock Performance Since ChatGPT Launched

When ChatGPT launched in November 2022, VRTX shares were trading at approximately $280. As of March 2026, the stock sits near $450, a gain of roughly 60% that comfortably outpaces the broader market's returns. The average 12-month analyst price target sits at approximately $537, implying further upside from current levels. Of 28 analysts covering the stock, the consensus rating is Buy.

The performance reflects not just Vertex's legacy CF business, but expanding confidence in its pipeline strategy. In early 2025, Vertex became the first drugmaker in 25 years to win FDA approval for a new class of pain medicine, with JOURNAVX (suzetrigine) — a non-opioid treatment — drawing over 500,000 prescriptions in its first year. In March 2026, its kidney disease drug povetacicept met the primary endpoint of a Phase 3 trial, opening the door to what analysts see as a major new franchise.

### The Compounding Advantage

What makes Vertex particularly compelling as an AI-era investment is the compounding nature of its advantages. Its CF revenues fund AI investment. Its AI investment accelerates pipeline development. Its pipeline diversification reduces single-product risk. And its proprietary real-world data from deployed therapies feeds back into better AI models.

With $11.4 billion in cash on its balance sheet as of mid-2025, Vertex has the financial firepower to keep making the large, long-horizon bets that AI-driven drug discovery demands — a luxury unavailable to smaller AI-native biotechs that must constantly return to capital markets.

### Risks Worth Noting

CF market saturation remains a genuine long-term concern. There are only so many eligible patients. JOURNAVX and CASGEVY are meaningful but still developing revenue contributors, and some analysts have flagged that their early ramp has been slower than peak optimism suggested. Clinical risk remains ever-present; even a well-run pipeline produces trial failures.

But for investors looking for a company that has genuinely integrated AI into its commercial and scientific DNA, with a proven revenue base, a maturing pipeline, and a data moat that will only deepen, Vertex stands as one of the more credible stories in biotechnology today.
