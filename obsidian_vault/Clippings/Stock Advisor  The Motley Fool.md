## TAKE-TWO INTERACTIVE SOFTWARE (NASDAQ:TTWO)

Most people think of Take-Two as a video game company.

They're not wrong. But that framing undersells what's actually happening inside this organization. Because the next 18 months could represent a genuine opportunity for investors.

Here's the first thing you need to understand: Take-Two is in the business of building worlds. Not games. Worlds. And with AI now embedded across its development pipeline, those worlds are becoming richer, more responsive, and more profitable than anything we've ever seen in entertainment history.

CEO Strauss Zelnick didn't mince words on the company's most recent earnings call. "We have hundreds of AI projects running now," he told investors. Not one. Not a pilot program. Hundreds. That's the scale of investment happening behind the scenes at one of the world's largest interactive entertainment companies.

And the numbers are starting to reflect it. In the third quarter of fiscal 2026, Take-Two reported net bookings of $1.76 billion, a 28% jump year-over-year that beat the company's own guidance. Management promptly raised its full-year outlook to a range of $6.65 to $6.7 billion. This is a business firing on all cylinders before its biggest catalyst has even arrived.

That catalyst, of course, is Grand Theft Auto VI.

Scheduled for release on November 19, 2026, GTA VI is arguably the most anticipated entertainment release in history. No film, no album, no streaming premiere has ever generated this level of pre-release economic attention. Analysts at Raymond James, who recently upgraded TTWO to Strong Buy, believe the title's launch will establish a new financial baseline for the business entirely. Management has guided for *record* net bookings in fiscal year 2027 — the year the game drops.

But here's what most investors miss: the real money in Take-Two's model isn't the box office moment when GTA VI launches. It's the years of recurring revenue that follow it.

Take a look at recurrent consumer spending — the in-game purchases, virtual currency, and subscription revenue that flows in long after the initial sale. In Q3 of fiscal 2026, recurrent spending accounted for 76% of Take-Two's total net bookings. Think about that. Three-quarters of the company's revenue today comes from games that were already sold. GTA V, released in 2013, still generates meaningful revenue in 2026. NBA 2K26 has a loyal, paying base that renews its habits every single year. Zynga's mobile portfolio — which includes perennial hits like Toon Blast — prints cash with the consistency of a toll booth.

Now layer AI on top of that model.

AI-assisted game development is allowing Take-Two's studios to create content faster and at lower cost than at any point in the company's history. Dynamic NPC behavior, procedurally generated environments, AI-driven narrative branching — these aren't hypothetical future features. They're being built right now, in-house, across hundreds of active projects. The implication for margins is significant. Less development time means lower costs. Lower costs on a growing revenue base mean expanding profitability.

And then there's the metaverse angle — one that gets dismissed too quickly by skeptics who associate the word with Meta's failed VR gamble. Take-Two's open-world games are already functioning proto-metaverses. Rockstar's servers host tens of millions of concurrent users in persistent, commerce-enabled digital environments. As AI makes those environments more dynamic and those digital economies more sophisticated, the line between "game" and "online world" disappears entirely.

The near-term picture is equally compelling. Institutional investors have been buying every dip, with 78 hedge fund portfolios holding TTWO at the end of Q4.

For investors who want exposure to the AI-media revolution through a proven, cash-generating franchise with a world-class catalyst on the horizon Take-Two is an interesting opportunity.

## SPOTIFY (NYSE:SPOT)

There's a joke that Spotify's relationship with musicians is complicated.

It's not wrong. But investors who've been watching the business underneath the controversy have noticed something the critics keep missing: Spotify has quietly built the most sophisticated AI personalization engine in consumer media — and it's turning that engine into a moat.

Consider the numbers first. Spotify now has 751 million users across 184 markets, including 290 million paying subscribers. Spotify paid $11 billion to content creators in 2025 alone, and still had plenty left to reinvest in the product.

What are they investing in? AI. Specifically, AI-driven personalization at a scale no competitor can match.

At SXSW in March 2026, Co-CEO Gustav Söderström unveiled Taste Profile — a new feature that allows users to see exactly how Spotify's algorithms understand their listening behavior and actively shape it in real time. This builds on Prompted Playlists, which launched in late 2025 and allows users to describe what they want to hear in natural language, drawing on their *entire* listening history dating back to day one on the platform. Think of it as writing your own algorithm.

"If iDJ is the chat interface to Spotify," said Co-CEO Alex Norström, "Prompted Playlists is the Deep Research mode of Spotify."

The iDJ he referenced — Spotify's AI DJ feature — already has roughly 90 million subscribers using it. Users are racking up over four billion hours of time with the feature. For context: that's not background listening. That's active, engaged session time driven entirely by an AI model. And engagement is the lifeblood of a subscription business.

Here's the strategic insight that Wall Street is starting to price in: Spotify's music catalog is essentially identical to Apple Music, Amazon Music, and YouTube Music. Any song you want to hear, you can hear on all four platforms. So what keeps 751 million people coming back to Spotify specifically?

The algorithm.

Spotify appears positioned to use AI to strengthen its platform as the core content — music — becomes increasingly commoditized across streaming services. The moat isn't the content. The moat is the intelligence layer sitting on top of it.

And Spotify is widening that moat with new integrations every quarter. A ChatGPT partnership now allows users to invoke Spotify directly within ChatGPT conversations for personalized recommendations. The company has formed a generative AI research lab alongside major labels — Sony, Universal, and Warner — tasked with developing new creation tools for artists that could unlock entirely new revenue streams. Its AI ad-tech platform, the Spotify Audience Network, allows advertisers to target listeners based on real-time activity context — cooking, running, commuting — with a level of precision that traditional broadcast media can never replicate.

The company is aiming to build the first truly intelligent media platform.

If that sounds like a bold claim for what started as a music streaming app, consider this: Spotify has already moved deeply into podcasts, audiobooks, and video content. Its multi-format strategy means it captures listener attention across every context of the day. Morning commute, afternoon workout, evening wind-down — Spotify wants to be the audio layer for all of it, with AI making each experience feel personal rather than algorithmic.

More than 80% of Spotify listeners cite personalization as what they love most about the platform. When your biggest competitive advantage is also the thing AI makes exponentially better, the thesis writes itself.

## THE WALT DISNEY COMPANY (NYSE:DIS)

For much of the past five years, Disney's story has been a survival story.

Linear TV declining. Streaming losses mounting. Iger back at the helm trying to right the ship before the creditors started circling. It made for gripping headlines — but it wasn't exactly an investor's dream.

That story is over.

What's emerging in its place is something genuinely exciting: a media empire that has stopped fighting the AI wave and started riding it.

Disney's Office of Technology Enablement — its internal "AI brain" — reached full operational scale in late 2025, coordinating more than 100 experts across Studios, Parks, and Streaming. In 2025, Disney's Accelerator program selected Animaj, an AI startup that can reduce animated episode production from five months to under five weeks. Disney also unveiled its Disney Select AI Engine for precision advertising targeting and Magic Words Live, a real-time contextual ad-matching tool.

Meanwhile, the streaming business itself is healing fast. Streaming revenue grew 13% year-over-year, driven by subscriber growth and bundle adoption. Disney's management has set a target of reaching a 10% streaming margin in fiscal 2026 — a number that would have seemed wildly optimistic as recently as 2023. CEO Bob Iger said the integrated Disney+/Hulu bundle has already "resulted in a reduction in churn," and a fully unified app experience is on track for later in 2026.

The long game here is one of the most compelling in media. Disney's IP library is among the most valuable private collections of intellectual property on the planet. As AI makes content generation cheaper, faster, and more democratized, every piece of that library becomes *more* valuable — because it's the only thing that can't be replicated. No AI can create the emotional bond audiences have with Mickey Mouse, or Luke Skywalker, or Elsa. But AI can help Disney monetize those bonds in ways that were never possible before.

Add in ESPN's digital expansion, the vertical video push coming to Disney+ over the next 12 months, and continued recovery in theme park operations, and the picture is of a company whose best years genuinely may still be ahead of it.

## ROKU (NASDAQ:ROKU)

Roku has always been the Switzerland of streaming wars.

While Netflix, Disney, and Amazon fought over content supremacy, Roku quietly became the infrastructure those wars run on. Today, with 90 million streaming households and a 37% share of the connected TV device market in North America, Roku isn't just a platform — it's the platform. More U.S. viewing time happens on Roku-powered devices than on traditional broadcast television.

And that position becomes more valuable, not less, as AI reshapes the advertising industry.

Here's the thesis: AI is cannibalizing search and social advertising. AI summaries are replacing traditional search results, causing traffic and ad volume to decline on Google. Content farms are flooding social media with AI-generated noise, creating brand safety minefields for marketers. Advertisers who built their performance marketing on search and social are looking for somewhere else to go.

That somewhere is connected TV — and Roku's prediction, backed by IAB data, is that up to 50% of streaming advertisers will redirect budget from search and social into CTV in 2026. Already, 36% of advertisers planning to increase CTV spend say they'll pull those dollars from social. Another 32% say they'll pull from paid search. The migration is underway.

Roku's financial results confirm the momentum. For the full year 2025, platform revenue — the segment that includes advertising — grew 18% year-over-year to $4.15 billion. Q4 alone was a record $1.22 billion. Roku says its video advertising growth outpaced the broader OTT and digital ad markets in the U.S. CEO Anthony Wood used a February 2026 earnings call to tell investors he expects generative AI to significantly reduce content production costs, which would drive more content creation and therefore more streaming consumption — a direct tailwind for Roku's platform.

The company's AI investments are internal as well as structural. Roku readied an AI-powered voice discovery interface, giving viewers a smarter way to navigate the platform. Its Ads Manager is deploying AI creative tools that the company says will help local advertisers boost ad effectiveness by up to 30% compared to linear TV. A major partnership with Amazon Ads — described by Roku executives as still in "early innings" — aims to create the largest authenticated CTV audience footprint in the U.S.

The data advantage Roku holds over rivals is underappreciated. Because Roku owns the operating system, the hardware, and The Roku Channel itself, it has direct first-party billing relationships with its users. In a world where third-party cookies are disappearing and AI is degrading the targeting precision of search and social, Roku's first-party data is a genuine competitive moat. Its Automatic Content Recognition technology can identify what viewers are watching across both streaming and traditional TV — enabling advertiser capabilities that simply don't exist anywhere else.

The bottom line: Roku is the toll road of the streaming revolution. It doesn't need to win the content wars. It just needs people to keep watching — and then help the advertisers who want to reach those people do so more efficiently than any other platform can.

Given where the ad dollar migration is heading, Roku's runway looks long.

## THE NEW YORK TIMES COMPANY (NYSE:NYT)

The conventional wisdom says AI is an existential threat to journalism.

The New York Times isn't waiting around to find out if the conventional wisdom is right. It's suing AI companies, building proprietary products that AI can't replicate, and quietly assembling one of the most durable digital subscription businesses in media — with 12.78 million paying subscribers and a clear line of sight to 15 million by the end of 2027.

Let's start with the moat, because it's the most important part of the NYT thesis.

The Times isn't just a newspaper. It's a bundle. News, The Athletic, Cooking, Games, Wirecutter, and Audio — six distinct products, all wrapped into a single subscription that deepens with every passing year. CEO Meredith Kopit Levien has described this strategy plainly: "We are confident in our ability to generate strong returns as we grow the amount and impact of video journalism in news and across our portfolio."

The numbers back her up. In Q4 2025, digital advertising revenue grew 24.9% year-over-year to $147.2 million. Digital-only subscription revenue grew 13.9% in the same period. For the full year, total revenue came in at $2.825 billion — a 9.24% increase — with management guiding for $3 billion in 2026. That's a business compounding reliably in a macro environment that's been brutal for most ad-supported media.

The AI angle cuts both ways here, and intellectual honesty demands acknowledging both sides.

On the risk side: the Times spent $13.3 million in 2025 on litigation costs related to AI companies training their models on Times content without authorization. That lawsuit — primarily against OpenAI — is among the most closely watched media-industry legal battles of the decade, and its outcome could reshape how AI companies compensate content creators for years to come. The Times is fighting that battle aggressively.

On the opportunity side, the Times is using AI internally to enhance productivity and accessibility in ways that make its journalism more scalable. AI tools are helping reporters surface information faster, making its products more responsive to reader behavior, and supporting the advertising targeting capabilities that drove that 24.9% digital ad growth. The company's position is clear: differentiated, premium journalism is *more* valuable in an AI world, not less, because AI cannot replicate the trust and expertise that the Times brand represents.

There's also a game-theory element here that sophisticated investors should consider. If the Times wins — or settles favorably — in its AI litigation, it establishes a licensing framework that could generate recurring revenue from every major AI company that wants access to premium journalism. The Times would effectively become an infrastructure supplier to the very industry that threatened to disrupt it.

Meanwhile, the bundle strategy continues to reduce churn and raise ARPU (average revenue per user). Games and Cooking alone have become habit-forming daily products for millions of subscribers. The Athletic gives sports fans a reason to stay even if they read less hard news. Wordle — free and viral — keeps the funnel full. Each product deepens the relationship between the subscriber and the bundle, making cancellation harder to justify.

In a media landscape being upended by AI, the companies that survive will be the ones with genuine human trust at their core. For 170 years, the New York Times has built that trust. That's not something a language model can hallucinate into existence.
