## Quant Metrics, Explained

What to know about our quant model categories, investing types, and drawdown/returns estimates.

The Fool's Quant model considers a variety of business metrics to gauge the investment potential of each company on its own merits. But that can't tell you whether a business is the right investment for *you.*

We encourage all members to understand the detail behind these scores and to also consider them in the broader context of your investment goals, existing portfolio, and risk preferences.

The score is broken into several categories to illustrate how our data-driven models "feel" about the stock. A stock's strength in each category is represented by percentiles -- the higher the number, the stronger the positive signal.

Each category includes a group of business metrics -- called score drivers -- that represents a relatable theme. While our category definitions don't actually affect the models, they help us generate insights from the results (and they might change in the future as we add new drivers).

Here are our current categories:

### Quality

A company's ability to generate a return on investment. We want reliable profits on a lean asset base. Our quality drivers capture these traits across a wide range of business models.

- Gross Profits / Assets
- Cash Flow / Assets
- Return on Invested Capital
- Net Accruals / Assets -- Lower is better
- Net Payout / Profits

### Growth

The compounding ability of a business. We measure the growth and investment trends because we want to identify the businesses positioned for high future growth potential.

- Gross Margin Growth
- Growth of Gross Profit / Assets
- Growth of Cash Flow / Assets
- Future Growth Optionality
- R&D Spending / Gross Profits
- Stock-Based Compensation / Gross Profits

### Safety

How well a company mitigates risks to its business. We track a variety of solvency, cash flow volatility, and market measures of risk. We want to avoid anything unnecessarily risky.

- Net Debt / EBITDA -- Lower is better
- Earnings Volatility -- Lower is better
- Leverage -- Lower is better

### Valuation

Measures of the richness or cheapness of a firm as it relates to different fundamental business metrics, pursuing an optimal mix of quality and value. We don't want to pay astronomical prices for future growth.

- Gross Profits / Enterprise Value
- Cash Flow / Enterprise Value
- Adjusted Book Value / Price

### Market Buzz

While harder to describe, this boils down to a company's current or growing presence in the market zeitgeist. Does it transcend an industry? Has it become a verb? Indications of powerful adoption trends can present themselves before they show up in company financial results. When the market is excited about a company, the business gains a better chance of realizing its true value.

- Momentum
- Liquidity Trend
- Liquidity Stability

## Investing Types, Explained

As a Motley Fool member, you have access to hundreds of active recommendations (with more being added each month). That's a lot of companies to consider when building out a well-diversified portfolio of at least 50 stocks that you're comfortable holding for five or more years.

It's not always easy to decide which stocks fit your investing needs. So we're here to help!

**Investing Types** are designed to help you navigate the options, along with the rest of our Quant projections.

We've identified three core Investing Types that correspond with risk tolerance and potential returns: **Cautious**, **Moderate**, and **Aggressive**.

Using simulations run by our Quant team, we've assigned a type to each stock in the Motley Fool universe, which you'll see on the new Epic experience and in Fool IQ. In general, the greater the range of possible outcomes for a stock, the more volatile (and more aggressive) our model expects it to be.

### Cautious

Not the flashiest stocks on a ticker feed, companies in this group are likely to have a limited range of return outcomes. But what they lack in 10-bagger potential, they make up for with lower potential risk.

### Moderate

The middle ground. These stocks have more growth potential than those in the Cautious category but a smaller range of return outcomes and less potential risk than their Aggressive counterparts.

### Aggressive

Outsized return potential comes with a cost: more risk. These stocks feature the widest range of outcomes -- increased chances of volatility, highest return potential -- and represent the rockiest road for an investor to navigate.

### How to Use Investing Types

We encourage all investors to understand their risk tolerance preferences and how that relates to the type of stocks they might want to own -- or not own. Only you know the type of investments that are best for your financial situation.

But we do recommend creating a diversified portfolio as one of the keys to successful long-term investing. Regardless of any individual preferences, we believe that investors should generally include stocks of all three Investing Types in their portfolios.

Even Aggressive stocks can be safely included (in smaller amounts) in a Cautious portfolio. Likewise, Cautious stocks can be an important segment when added to a more Aggressive portfolio.

We recommend members review our Portfolio Strategies, which offer three versions (Aggressive, Moderate, and Cautious) to illustrate how stocks across all three Investing Types can be combined into a diversified, risk-managed portfolio.

### How Are Investing Types Determined?

1. Our Quant team uses our **internal multifactor model** -- [weighing different "factors"](https://www.fool.com/terms/f/factor-investing/) of financial metrics and market trends -- to predict each company's potential range of return over the next year. Each prediction is based on thousands of simulations.
2. Potential returns aren't considered in a vacuum. All of the forecast data together creates what we call a **Market Map**. It represents the simulated returns of all the stocks in the Motley Fool universe and how likely they are to correlate with one another.
3. The team calculates a **risk measurement** for each stock**,** focused on potential downside, by examining how much and how frequently a stock's price went down within the simulated forecasts.
4. Stocks in the Motley Fool's recommendation universe are **assigned one of the three Investing Types** by the risk measurement. Those with higher numbers land in Aggressive, lower in Cautious, with Moderate in between.

### Can a Stock's Investing Type Change?

Yes! As a company evolves, we anticipate its Investing Type category changing as well. But this won't necessarily be a quick or frequent change.

Think of how businesses evolve. Some move from start-up to established profit generator, while others might be dividend-paying stalwarts rocked by disruption in their space. In most cases, change happens over months, business quarters, and years -- not overnight.

We expect to update our Investing Type datasets twice a year. While not every company will see a shift, some will. Categories listed in stock tables and tools on the site will reflect the current type, while recommendation articles will include the type as of the date of publication.

## Estimated Drawdown and Estimated Returns, Explained

We're Fools. We are providing annualized return and max-downside estimates -- powered by our quant investing intelligence team -- as additional context on the types of risks and opportunities one might expect with an individual stock or collection of stocks.

These estimates and projections are *not* guaranteed. These estimates will be incorrect sometimes, as should be expected with any estimates about the future.

So use these estimates as an additional tool in the toolkit, but understand that they are not perfect, and they will be wrong at times along the way. An investment decision should not be based solely on any one number like these.

These numbers are estimates of probability, so they might be wrong because of an error in our model or the underlying data. Alternatively, we could be absolutely correct about our model, but the actual performance could be one of the less probable outcomes than the estimated range we publish.

We strive for the data, model, and estimates to be accurate and up to date, but please be aware there may be instances in which the process or outputs could be inaccurate or incomplete.

We expect and aim for the estimates to be updated at least once per year, but that is not guaranteed, and the frequency of updates could shift.

Our commitment is to continue improving these tools and insights to help our members make more informed investment decisions. We are Fools, and we hope you find these tools and insights useful on your financial journey.

### How to Use These Metrics

**To understand risk and reward:** What kind of ride should you buckle up for if you decide to add shares to your portfolio? Our quant return and risk estimates are designed to give you a snapshot of a stock's likely performance characteristics for the year ahead.

**To help decide if a stock is right for you:** What's more important than buying the right stock? Sometimes, it's *not buying* the wrong stock! Because our estimates are designed to provide you with our quant team's "best guess" at a stock's likely return stats, they can help you identify stocks that don't fit well with your investor type.

When you're evaluating a stock for your portfolio, if you're not comfortable with seeing annual returns that fall within our expected return range or potentially enduring a one-year drawdown on the scale of our estimated maximum drawdown, the stock might not be right for you.

Likewise, our quant estimates can also provide a useful comparison if you're trying to decide between a pair of potential investments.

**To put recommendations in context:** Because our quant return and risk estimates are generated independently of our analyst team's recommendations, they can offer some interesting context and insight into one of our Foolish recommendations.

For instance, a recommendation of a stock with a lower expected return band could suggest that our Foolish analyst team is forecasting a meaningful change to the business. But it also means that you should be prepared for returns to fall within that lower band before the investment thesis pays off.

**With a grain of salt:** Like any market estimates, our quant metrics are subject to error, revisions, and statistical noise. We've designed these metrics to provide useful additional context in a weight-of-the-evidence approach to investing -- but they're not meant to perfectly predict the future.

### How These Metrics Are Calculated

Here are more details on how these numbers are calculated:

**Market Map:** Market Map is a high-dimensional probability distribution of year-ahead returns for about 3,000 stocks. In essence, we are forecasting what the potential range of outcomes (returns) may be for each stock and forming a distribution based on those estimates. Once this process is completed for each constituent in our estimation universe, we have something that looks like the example below (although not quite so normal looking) for each of our ~3,000 stocks.

![Image showing Market Map's Estimated Year-Ahead Annualized Distribution curve.](https://g.foolcdn.com/editorial/images/762573/1-market-map_large.png)

This then provides the basis for the estimation of a variety of stock-related statistics (estimated annualized return ranges, expected max drawdowns, etc.).

**Estimated Annualized Return Range:** The estimated return ranges come from our internal Market Map, which is a high-dimensional probability distribution of year-ahead returns for about 3,000 stocks. The ranges we're currently displaying are the 40th and 60th percentiles of those probability distributions for each stock. These provide an approximate estimate of return expectations one year out while capturing some of the uncertainty inherent in a particular stock.

![Image showing Market Map's Estimated Year-Ahead Annualized Distribution curve with 40th and 60th percentiles marked.](https://g.foolcdn.com/editorial/images/762573/2-est-year-ahead_large.png)

**Estimated Max Drawdown:** We also estimate max drawdowns using our Market Map. In this case, we use an analytical estimator of the expected max drawdown under some simplifying assumptions (namely that log-returns follow a Brownian motion with drift), using the Market Map to estimate some parameters.

Essentially, we want to take what we know about a stock's distribution of returns and try to estimate what the year-ahead magnitude of a max drawdown might be. Like the historical example below, we are trying to estimate what the magnitude of the drawdown might look like for any given stock in the next year.

### Want More Color?

**Here it is, explained with some help from ChatGPT:**

**Annualized Return Ranges:** Think of this as a video game in which you can choose from 3,000 paths (each path is a stock). The Market Map is like a guide that shows how well each path might do in a year. The 40th and 60th percentiles are like markers along each path. If you're at the 40th marker, you're doing better than many paths but not as well as others. This helps you guess how much money you might earn or lose in a year on each path.

**Drawdowns:** This is like predicting the biggest scary drop on a roller coaster (which represents how much a stock's value might fall). Market Map helps make this prediction using what we know about an individual stock's return pattern through time (distribution) and some assumptions about the statistical properties of how returns generally behave. It's like trying to guess the scariest part of the roller coaster ride before you get on it.
