## Buffett’s Investment Methodology

Warren Buffett finds low-priced value by asking himself some questions when he evaluates the relationship between a stock's level of excellence and its price.13 But keep in mind that these aren't the only things he analyzes. They make a brief summary of what he looks for in his six-step investment approach.

Return on equity (ROE) is sometimes referred to as the stockholders’ return on investment. It reveals the rate at which shareholders earn income on their shares. Buffett always looks at ROE to see whether a company has consistently performed well when compared to other companies in the same industry.14

ROE is calculated as follows:

**ROE = (Net Income ÷ Shareholder’s Equity) × 100**

Looking at the ROE in just the last year isn’t enough. An investor should view it from the past five to 10 years to analyze historical performance.

The [debt-to-equity (D/E) ratio](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/d/debtequityratio.asp) is another characteristic Buffett considers carefully. He prefers to see a small amount of debt with earnings growth being generated from shareholders’ equity rather than borrowed money.14

The D/E ratio is calculated as follows:

**Debt-to-Equity Ratio = Total Liabilities ÷ Shareholders' Equity**

This ratio shows the proportion of equity and debt the company uses to finance its assets. The higher the ratio, the more debt the company uses to finance itself rather than equity. A high debt level compared to equity can result in volatile earnings and large interest expenses, but some industries require higher D/E ratios. Investors sometimes use only long-term debt instead of total liabilities in the above calculation for a more stringent test.
