a business running on 3–4% operating margins, shaving costs through AI in logistics, inventory, and labor scheduling is not a luxury. It is essential to the flywheel.

### The Physical Moat Is Durable

Perhaps the deepest protection Costco has against AI disruption is the one that gets the least attention: the sheer physical reality of its warehouses. Costco operates over 900 locations worldwide, and its model depends on members driving to stores, buying in bulk, and loading up their cars. This is not a bug — it is the feature. The treasure hunt experience, the famous $1.50 hot dog, the free samples, the sense of discovery — these are sensory, physical experiences that no AI-powered e-commerce interface has come close to replicating.

Amazon has tried to crack the warehouse club model for years and has not meaningfully dented Costco's membership base. If the world's most sophisticated e-commerce and AI company cannot disrupt Costco at scale, the disruption risk from AI alone looks modest.

### Risks Worth Acknowledging

No investment thesis is complete without the counterargument. Costco's institutional culture — famously people-centric, promote-from-within — can be slow to adapt to new technology. Competitors like Sam's Club and Amazon are investing aggressively in frictionless, digitally integrated shopping experiences. If AI shopping agents eventually reduce the friction of online bulk purchasing to the point that consumers stop making the trip, Costco's e-commerce capabilities — still well behind Amazon and Walmart — could become a liability.

The tariff environment in early 2026 also presents a near-term headwind, with Costco among the retailers seeking reimbursement for tariff-related cost increases. And while e-commerce now represents a growing share of revenue, the company still lags its largest peers in digital infrastructure.
