## Regulatory Advantage

Competitive advantages that result from regulation usually stem from some [government actions](https://www.investopedia.com/articles/economics/11/government-regulations.asp) that limit competitors from attacking. In Google's case, the company's market share in search and in its Android mobile operating system is so strong that regulation looms as a possible threat to the company's ability to maintain its strong profitability.

Regulators in the United States and particularly in Europe are monitoring Google's business practices for anti-competitive behavior. There may be no better evidence of the strength of Google's moat than regulators actually declaring it unfair to the competition.

On Oct. 20, 2020, the United States Department of Justice (DOJ)—along with the attorney generals in 11 states—filed a [civil antitrust lawsuit](https://www.investopedia.com/ask/answers/09/antitrust-law.asp) against Google, declaring the company unlawfully maintained monopolies via its anti-competitive practices in the search and search advertising markets. The lawsuit seeks to remedy Google's anticompetitive violations and restore competition to the marketplace. While the initial filing does not specify how the DOJ intends to accomplish this, it does point to other antitrust cases, such as those against [Standard Oil and AT&T](https://www.investopedia.com/insights/history-of-us-monopolies/), which resulted in the breakup of both companies.4

Looking forward, it is likely that Google's competitive advantages will have to sustain themselves in spite of regulation, rather than as a result of it.
