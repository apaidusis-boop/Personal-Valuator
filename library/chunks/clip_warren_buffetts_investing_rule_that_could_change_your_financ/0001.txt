In practice, that means focusing less on hot trends or complex strategies and more on how a business actually works—how it makes money, who its customers are, and whether it can grow over time.

Buffett has followed this approach consistently, investing in companies like Coca-Cola ([KO](https://www.investopedia.com/markets/quote?tvwidgetsymbol=KO)) and See’s Candies while steering clear of areas he didn’t fully understand, including much of the dot-com boom.

“Buffett’s style keeps you focused on what the business does and why it matters,” Pamela Sams, a financial advisor at Jackson Sams Wealth Strategies, told Investopedia. “That’s how you avoid mistakes and busted portfolios.”

## How to Use Buffett’s Rule When Choosing Investments

Applying Buffett’s rule starts with focusing on what you already know.

Begin by identifying industries, products, or services you’re familiar with—whether through your work, interests, or everyday experience.2 From there, narrow your focus to a smaller group of companies and dig into the details—looking at their financials, leadership, and long-term strategy.

“People [chase trends](https://www.investopedia.com/buffett-s-guide-to-controlling-your-investments-and-outsmarting-mr-market-11875047) or fall for flashy numbers, but Buffett knows that revenue is meaningless if expenses are out of control, and leadership is critical for success,” Sams said.

Buffett’s investment in Apple Inc. ([AAPL](https://www.investopedia.com/markets/quote?tvwidgetsymbol=AAPL)) reflects this approach. He initially avoided tech, but later viewed Apple as a consumer-focused business with strong brand loyalty—something he could confidently understand and evaluate.
