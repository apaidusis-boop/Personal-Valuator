It’s also quietly become a meaningful e-commerce player, with online sales now contributing a growing share of revenue. At the same time, Costco continues to expand its physical footprint, opening 20 to 30 new warehouses each year across both domestic and international markets.

The typical Costco shopper earns around $125,000 annually. That gives the business a high-quality base of customers who tend to keep spending, even when the economy tightens.

Costco isn’t in the business of chasing hype. It’s in the business of keeping people happy, saving them money, and delivering steady growth over time. That makes it a valuable anchor in any long-term portfolio.

## 2\. TJX COMPANIES (NYSE: TJX)

TJX is one of the best businesses most investors overlook. It owns brands like T.J. Maxx, Marshalls, HomeGoods, and Winners, delivering name-brand clothing, home furnishings, and essentials at prices traditional retailers can’t match.

And while other chains struggle with inventory or discounting, TJX thrives in precisely that environment.

That’s because its entire model is built around opportunistic buying. Over 1,300 professional buyers are constantly on the hunt for excess inventory and markdowns from brands and suppliers, allowing TJX to offer deals year-round without running heavy promotions.

The result is a business that performs well when consumers are spending freely, and arguably even better when they start pulling back. When shoppers look to stretch their dollars, they often turn to off-price retail.

That resilience shows up in the numbers. While many retailers took a hit during recent market pullbacks, TJX stock has proven durable. In fact, when the S&P 500 dropped nearly 19 percent in early 2025, TJX shares declined just 5 percent.
