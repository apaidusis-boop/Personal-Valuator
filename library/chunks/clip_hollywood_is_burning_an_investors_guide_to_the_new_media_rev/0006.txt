And that position becomes more valuable, not less, as AI reshapes the advertising industry.

Here's the thesis: AI is cannibalizing search and social advertising. AI summaries are replacing traditional search results, causing traffic and ad volume to decline on Google. Content farms are flooding social media with AI-generated noise, creating brand safety minefields for marketers. Advertisers who built their performance marketing on search and social are looking for somewhere else to go.

That somewhere is connected TV — and Roku's prediction, backed by IAB data, is that up to 50% of streaming advertisers will redirect budget from search and social into CTV in 2026. Already, 36% of advertisers planning to increase CTV spend say they'll pull those dollars from social. Another 32% say they'll pull from paid search. The migration is underway.

Roku's financial results confirm the momentum. For the full year 2025, platform revenue — the segment that includes advertising — grew 18% year-over-year to $4.15 billion. Q4 alone was a record $1.22 billion. Roku says its video advertising growth outpaced the broader OTT and digital ad markets in the U.S. CEO Anthony Wood used a February 2026 earnings call to tell investors he expects generative AI to significantly reduce content production costs, which would drive more content creation and therefore more streaming consumption — a direct tailwind for Roku's platform.
