The company's AI investments are internal as well as structural. Roku readied an AI-powered voice discovery interface, giving viewers a smarter way to navigate the platform. Its Ads Manager is deploying AI creative tools that the company says will help local advertisers boost ad effectiveness by up to 30% compared to linear TV. A major partnership with Amazon Ads — described by Roku executives as still in "early innings" — aims to create the largest authenticated CTV audience footprint in the U.S.

The data advantage Roku holds over rivals is underappreciated. Because Roku owns the operating system, the hardware, and The Roku Channel itself, it has direct first-party billing relationships with its users. In a world where third-party cookies are disappearing and AI is degrading the targeting precision of search and social, Roku's first-party data is a genuine competitive moat. Its Automatic Content Recognition technology can identify what viewers are watching across both streaming and traditional TV — enabling advertiser capabilities that simply don't exist anywhere else.

The bottom line: Roku is the toll road of the streaming revolution. It doesn't need to win the content wars. It just needs people to keep watching — and then help the advertisers who want to reach those people do so more efficiently than any other platform can.

Given where the ad dollar migration is heading, Roku's runway looks long.
