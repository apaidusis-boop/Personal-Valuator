as mortgages, [credit cards](https://www.investopedia.com/how-do-credit-cards-work-5025119), or insurance policies. This is often done through strategic partnerships rather than advertising networks.

### Important

When [evaluating a robo-advisor](https://www.investopedia.com/articles/financial-advisors/032415/how-evaluate-roboadvisor-schw.asp), pay attention to what asset classes and ETFs it invests in, as some are now moving away from passive index strategies and investing in somewhat riskier areas that could either outperform or underperform the market, such as " [smart-beta](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/smart-beta.asp) " strategies.

## The Bottom Line

Robo-advisors leverage advances in algorithmic trading and electronic markets to [automate investment strategies](https://www.investopedia.com/automate-your-wealth-simply-with-a-robo-advisor-11778718) for ordinary investors.

Often based on modern portfolio theory, robo-advisors are able to optimize investors' [risk-return tradeoffs](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/r/riskreturntradeoff.asp) and automatically manage and rebalance their portfolios. Automation also allows for tax-loss harvesting and other strategies that were once too complex or expensive for ordinary investors.

With low fees and [small minimum balances](https://www.investopedia.com/how-much-should-you-invest-with-a-robo-advisor-11777248) required to get started, [robo-advisors may be a good choice](https://www.investopedia.com/m1-finance-vs-wealthfront-4693443) for most long-term investors and may be especially attractive to younger, tech-forward individuals.
