When liquidity is limited, as it often is in many stocks in emerging markets, the betas
estimated using short return intervals tend to be much more biased. In fact, using daily or
even weekly returns in these markets will tend to yield betas that are not good measures of
the true market risk of the company.
In many emerging markets, both the companies being analyzed and the market itself change
significantly over short periods of time. Using five years of returns, as we did for Boeing,
for a regression may yield a beta for a company (and market) that bears little resemblance to
the company (and market) as it exists today.
Finally, the indices that measure market returns in many smaller markets tend to be
dominated by a few large companies. For instance, the Bovespa (the Brazilian index) was
dominated for several years by Telebras, which represented almost half the index. Nor is
this just a problem with emerging markets. The DAX, the equity index for German stocks, is
dominated by Allianz, Deutsche Bank, Siemens, and Daimler. When an index is dominated
by one or a few companies, the betas estimated against that index are unlikely to be true
measures of market risk. In fact, the betas are likely to be close to 1 for the large companies
that dominate the index and wildly variable for all other companies.
