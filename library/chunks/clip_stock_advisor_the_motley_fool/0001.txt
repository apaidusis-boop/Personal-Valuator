But here's what most investors miss: the real money in Take-Two's model isn't the box office moment when GTA VI launches. It's the years of recurring revenue that follow it.

Take a look at recurrent consumer spending — the in-game purchases, virtual currency, and subscription revenue that flows in long after the initial sale. In Q3 of fiscal 2026, recurrent spending accounted for 76% of Take-Two's total net bookings. Think about that. Three-quarters of the company's revenue today comes from games that were already sold. GTA V, released in 2013, still generates meaningful revenue in 2026. NBA 2K26 has a loyal, paying base that renews its habits every single year. Zynga's mobile portfolio — which includes perennial hits like Toon Blast — prints cash with the consistency of a toll booth.

Now layer AI on top of that model.

AI-assisted game development is allowing Take-Two's studios to create content faster and at lower cost than at any point in the company's history. Dynamic NPC behavior, procedurally generated environments, AI-driven narrative branching — these aren't hypothetical future features. They're being built right now, in-house, across hundreds of active projects. The implication for margins is significant. Less development time means lower costs. Lower costs on a growing revenue base mean expanding profitability.

And then there's the metaverse angle — one that gets dismissed too quickly by skeptics who associate the word with Meta's failed VR gamble. Take-Two's open-world games are already functioning proto-metaverses. Rockstar's servers host tens of millions of concurrent users in persistent, commerce-enabled digital environments. As AI makes those environments more dynamic and those digital economies more sophisticated, the line between "game" and "online world" disappears entirely.
